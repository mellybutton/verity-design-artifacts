01
The prompt

Ask contrastively,
never directly.

The single highest-leverage design decision in the entire elicitation system is the wording of the BECAUSE prompt. Never ask "Why does this rule exist?" Ask instead: "What would go wrong if this rule didn't exist?"

Research basis: Nisbett & Wilson (1977) demonstrated that direct "why" questions trigger post-hoc rationalization — people construct plausible-sounding explanations after the fact rather than reporting actual reasoning. Lipton's contrastive theory of explanation (1990, 2004) established that "why P rather than Q?" activates genuine causal mechanism identification. CDM methodology independently found that expert-novice contrast probes ("What would a junior person get wrong here?") surface tacit knowledge that open prompting never reaches.
✓ Use

"What would go wrong if this rule didn't exist?"

✕ Never

"Why does this rule exist?" — invites rationalization, not reasoning.

The contrastive prompt also varies by card type. For AI draft review (where the AI attempted a BECAUSE and failed), the prompt shifts to critique framing: "Is this right? What's missing or wrong in the reasoning?" Critique mode is consistently more productive than authoring from scratch — it is easier to react to something than to create something.

AI draft — vague BECAUSE flagged
"This rule exists to ensure renewals are handled according to the correct process."
"Is this right? What's missing or wrong in the reasoning?"
Expert replacement
"Auto-processed renewals without a QBR have a 40% higher churn rate in the following year. The 60-day window is the last point where the conversation can influence renewal terms."

This prompt is locked. It should not be changed without a deliberate, documented decision. Every other element of the elicitation UX can be iterated — this one cannot be casually A/B tested, because the cognitive mechanism it activates is not a preference, it is a structural difference in how expert reasoning gets accessed.

02
The scaffold

Scaffold structure.
Never model content.

Optional scaffold prompts exist to help an expert who is stuck find a starting point — not to show them what a good BECAUSE looks like. The four scaffold prompts are dimensional questions, not examples:

Who does this protect, and from what? What would a junior person get wrong here? What evidence supports this threshold? What alternatives did you consider?
Research basis: Smith, Ward & Schumacher showed that example conformity persists even when participants are explicitly instructed to diverge. A single model BECAUSE in the UI will produce a library of BECAUSE fields that look like the example — regardless of what the expert actually knows. Show examples and you get mimicry. Show dimensional questions and you get reasoning.
✓ Use

Dimensional prompts that inject into the textarea on click. Hidden by default. Faded after first session.

✕ Never

Example BECAUSE fields, filled placeholder text, or "Here's a sample reasoning…" patterns anywhere in the UI.

Scaffold prompts are always hidden by default and expandable on request. They are never shown on every rule — only surfaced when the expert asks. The expert-novice contrast prompt ("What would a junior person get wrong here?") is particularly effective because it forces the expert to make implicit knowledge explicit. They cannot assume shared context when describing what a novice would miss.

03
The feedback

Coach inline.
Never block at save.

Quality feedback must appear while the expert is writing — not as a validation error when they try to submit. The goal is coaching, not gating. Two layers of feedback run in parallel:

Quality chips respond as the expert types, tracking three signals:

Names a consequence Names a stakeholder Specific enough
Names a consequence Names a stakeholder Specific enough

Chips are additive and encouraging — they light up when conditions are met, never turn red or penalize. The visual metaphor is completion, not error. Keyword-based at MVP; Claude-call quality detection in R2.

Inline nudges fire when specific failure modes are detected — never as a generic "add more detail" message. Two modes are computationally detectable at MVP:

Failure mode Detection signal Re-prompt
Tautology High semantic overlap between BECAUSE text and the IF/THEN, field length <90 chars "This restates the rule — what's the underlying reason it exists?"
Authority only Policy/regulation/compliance mention with no consequence language "You've named a requirement — can you name the specific risk it prevents?"
✓ Nudge tone

Curious, targeted, specific to the failure. "You mentioned SOX — what specific risk does it prevent in this context?"

✕ Never

"Please provide more detail." / "This field is required." / Red validation states that block submission.

In Slack (where live textarea events are unavailable), quality nudges fire post-submission as a follow-up DM — one nudge per rule maximum, never repeated. The expert can ignore it. The goal is surfacing the gap, not enforcing quality at the door.

04
The pacing

Protect the batch.
Don't schedule the session.

Expert reasoning quality degrades in predictable, measurable ways after 45 minutes of active writing. The UI must enforce cognitive budget constraints — but cannot ask the expert to change how they work. Scheduled sessions are a product nobody buys. Paced async is the answer.

Research basis: Israeli parole judge study (Danziger et al., PNAS 2011) showed favorable rulings dropped from ~65% at session start to near 0% by session end. SurveyMonkey data shows response time drops 75% from question 1 to question 26–30. The spacing effect (Cepeda et al. meta-analysis, 2006, 259 of 271 studies) confirms distributed practice outperforms massed practice with moderate effect size (d = 0.54). For BECAUSE authoring: 3–5 rules per session, 2–3 sessions per week, outperforms marathon runs.

The queue enforces pacing without announcing it. The expert checks Verity like they check Slack — pull-based, ambient, already fits how they work.

Mon
3 of 5 authored · done for today
Tue
2 authored · 1 in progress
Wed
Rest day — no queue surfaced
✓ Pacing rules

Max 5 rules per queue visit. "Pause for today" is a primary action. "Done for today" is an intentional state with explicit messaging about why spacing matters.

✕ Never

Surface all pending rules at once. Use "inbox zero" framing. Gamify streaks or completion rates. Guilt-trip incomplete sessions.

The done state copy is specific: "Returning tomorrow produces better reasoning — the spacing effect is real." This is not marketing language. It is true, and the expert deserves to know why the UI is behaving the way it is.

05
The incentive

Inactive until authored.
No exceptions.

A rule without a BECAUSE is saved to the library but cannot govern AI behavior. It is explicitly labelled "not active." This is not a UX pattern — it is a product constraint. It is the structural incentive that makes the entire elicitation system work.

The constraint exists for three reasons. First, a rule without reasoning is a constraint, not knowledge — and constraints without reasoning fail silently on novel cases. Second, it forces the attribution question: a rule with no BECAUSE has no author in any meaningful sense. Third, it gives the expert a clear, low-friction path to convert a draft into a live rule — the completion action is obvious and satisfying.

Missing reasoning · inactive Saved as draft · not active
⚠ No reasoning — this rule is not active. Add a BECAUSE to activate it.
✓ Framing

"Rules without a BECAUSE can't govern AI behavior." Clear consequence. Obvious fix. Non-judgmental.

✕ Never

"Incomplete." "Missing information." Warning icons without explanation. Blocking save entirely (experts must be able to capture first, reason later).

The escape hatch — "Save without reasoning" — must always exist. Blocking capture is worse than capturing without a BECAUSE. But the consequence must be visible and explicit: the rule will not run until it is complete.

06
The surfaces

Two surfaces.
One job each.

Verity operates across Slack and the web app. This is not a compromise — it is correct product design. Each surface has a distinct job, and neither should attempt to do the other's job.

💬
Slack
Capture surface
  • Rule extraction card posted in channel
  • BECAUSE modal on button click
  • Card updates in-place on confirm
  • DM nudges for queue and draft rules
  • Pacing enforced server-side (1 rule per DM thread)
Cannot do

Live quality chips · real-time nudges · library management · batch queue · incomplete rule enforcement

🖥
Web app
Quality coaching surface
  • Live quality chips while typing
  • Real-time tautology & authority nudges
  • Batch queue with pacing model
  • Library with filter, revision history
  • Incomplete rules view and inline fix
Cannot do

Meet the expert where they are · zero-tab capture · in-channel confirmation for the team to see

Architectural constraint: Desktop Slack runs as an Electron app. Browser extensions cannot touch it. Block Kit is the ceiling for in-message UI — Slack modals have no live textarea events. App Home (a dedicated tab in the Verity DM) is the furthest Slack-native surface available and can render queue/library stats in Block Kit, but not React. These constraints are permanent, not temporary.

The expert is in Slack when a rule moment surfaces — capture happens there. The expert chooses to open the web app when they are ready to do deliberate reasoning work — quality coaching belongs there. The job split is defined by where attention naturally lives, not by technical preference.

07
The authorship

Attribution is
the product.

Every confirmed rule carries its author's name and role. This is not metadata — it is the trust mechanism. When Morgan asks @verity a question and gets a rule card back, she sees Jordan's name. She knows whose judgment she is relying on. That is what makes the answer usable on a variant case, not just the exact case the rule was written for.

Attribution serves three functions simultaneously:

Trust. Rules with named authors are believed. Rules without authors are ignored or second-guessed. The library is only as useful as the confidence the team has in it.

Ownership. Jordan's name on every rule she confirms creates accountability without demanding it. She will not confirm a rule she disagrees with if her name is attached. Attribution is the quality gate that scales without enforcement.

Maintainability. When a rule needs to be reviewed, updated, or challenged, the attribution trail tells you who to ask. When the author leaves, it tells you what they owned and what needs a new maintainer. The knowledge gap that opens on departure is visible and bounded rather than invisible and diffuse.

✓ Always show

Author name + role on every rule card, confirmation state, and Slack response. Visible without expanding. Not in a tooltip.

✕ Never

"Authored by Verity." AI-attributed BECAUSE fields. Anonymous rules in any state. Attribution hidden behind an expand.

The BECAUSE field is always labelled as user-authored in the UI — not just in documentation. The label "Your reasoning here — the AI never fills this in" in the blank state is not a disclaimer. It is the product's core claim, stated at the moment of authorship.

From the failure modes research: The consensus trap — knowledge captured through group processes reflects consensus rather than the distribution of expert opinion. Named attribution is the countermeasure: it preserves whose judgment was captured, making it possible to assess whether the knowledge is applicable to a given context or whether the author's specific perspective should be considered.
